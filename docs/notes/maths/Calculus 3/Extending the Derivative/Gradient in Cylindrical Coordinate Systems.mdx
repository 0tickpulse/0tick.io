# Gradient of a Function in Cylindrical Coordinates

The gradient in cylindrical coordinates is defined a bit differently than in Cartesian coordinates.
Most notably, there is a $1/\class{red}{r}$ term in the $\class{purple}{\theta}$ component of the gradient.
We can use two ways to show this. First, we will provide an intuitive/visual explanation, and then we will provide an explicit algebraic proof.

## Intuitive Explanation

Recall that the gradient $f$ points in the direction of the steepest increase of $f$.
If we were to treat the surface of $f$ as something like a hill, the gradient would point in the direction that you would need to walk to go up the hill the fastest.

A prototype of the gradient in cylindrical coordinates could be:

$$
\begin{equation}
\grad{f} \overset{?}{=} \pdv{f}{\class{red}{r}} \class{red}{\vu{r}} + \pdv{f}{\class{purple}{\theta}} \class{purple}{\vu{\theta}} + \pdv{f}{\posZ} \class{green}{\vu{k}}
\end{equation}
$$

However this is not quite correct. Let's consider each term:

1. $\pdv{f}{\class{red}{r}} \class{red}{\vu{r}}$: This term is the rate of change of $f$ with respect to the distance from the origin.
2. $\pdv{f}{\posZ} \class{green}{\vu{k}}$: This term is the rate of change of $f$ with respect to the height from the $xy$-plane.
3. $\pdv{f}{\class{purple}{\theta}} \class{purple}{\vu{\theta}}$: This term is the rate of change of $f$ with respect to the angle from the $x$-axis.
    But wait! $\class{purple}{\theta}$ is a circular coordinate, meaning it is not a linear distance.
    It works a bit differently than the other two coordinates. We can illustrate this with a visual explanation.

Recall that we can interpret a derivative as the ratio of the change in the function to the change in the variable.

Draw two circles, one with radius $r$ and another with radius $2r$, and a point on both.
Let's change $\class{purple}{\theta}$ by a small amount $\Delta \class{purple}{\theta}$, and consider how it affects the output of both functions.

When we do this, take note:

1. As $\Delta \class{purple}{\theta} \to 0$, the change in the output of the function is approximately the arc length of the sector formed by the change in $\class{purple}{\theta}$.
2. As $\Delta \class{purple}{\theta} \to 0$, the direction of the change in the output of the function becomes tangent to the circle, i.e. $\class{purple}{\vu{\theta}}$.

Notice that the change in the point is proportional to the radius of the circle:

$$
\begin{equation}
\Delta \va{v} = \class{red}{r} \Delta \class{purple}{\theta} \, \class{purple}{\vu{\theta}}
\end{equation}
$$

The output of the function of this point is proportional to this change;

$$
\begin{equation}
\Delta f = \pdv{f}{\class{purple}{\theta}} \Delta \va{v} = \pdv{f}{\class{purple}{\theta}}\class{red}{r} \Delta \class{purple}{\theta} \, \class{purple}{\vu{\theta}}
\end{equation}
$$

In other words, as the radius increases, the change in the output of the function increases proportionally with the same change in $\class{purple}{\theta}$.
This is not the case for the other two coordinates, and is not captured by the gradient as we have defined it; it should be independent of the position on the space.

Another way to think of it is, instead of using the rate of change with respect to $\Delta \class{purple}{\theta}$, we should use the rate of change with respect to the corrected change $\Delta \class{purple}{\theta} / \class{red}{r}$.

Thus, we need to divide the rate of change of $f$ with respect to $\class{purple}{\theta}$ by the radius to account for this effect:

$$
\begin{equation}
\grad{f} = \pdv{f}{\class{red}{r}} \class{red}{\vu{r}} + \frac{1}{\class{red}{r}} \pdv{f}{\class{purple}{\theta}} \class{purple}{\vu{\theta}} + \pdv{f}{\posZ} \class{green}{\vu{k}}
\end{equation}
$$

## Explicit Proof

First, recall that the gradient of a function $f$ in Cartesian coordinates is:

$$
\begin{equation}
\grad{f} = \pdv{f}{\posX} \class{blue}{\vu{i}} + \pdv{f}{\posY} \class{yellow}{\vu{j}} + \pdv{f}{\posZ} \class{green}{\vu{k}}
\end{equation}
$$

Substitute in the equations for $\pdv{f}{\posX}$, $\pdv{f}{\posY}$, $\class{blue}{\vu{i}}$, $\class{yellow}{\vu{j}}$, and $\class{green}{\vu{k}}$ in terms of spherical coordinates:

$$
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
\grad{f} &= \pdv{f}{\posX} \class{blue}{\vu{i}} + \pdv{f}{\posY} \class{yellow}{\vu{j}} + \pdv{f}{\posZ} \class{green}{\vu{k}} \\
&= \qty(\pdv{f}{\class{red}{r}} \cos(\class{purple}{\theta}) - \pdv{f}{\class{purple}{\theta}} \frac{\sin(\class{purple}{\theta})}{\class{red}{r}})\class{blue}{\vu{i}} + \qty(\pdv{f}{\class{red}{r}} \sin(\class{purple}{\theta}) + \pdv{f}{\class{purple}{\theta}} \frac{\cos(\class{purple}{\theta})}{\class{red}{r}})\class{yellow}{\vu{j}} + \pdv{f}{\posZ} \class{green}{\vu{k}} \\
&= \qty(\pdv{f}{\class{red}{r}} \cos(\class{purple}{\theta}) - \pdv{f}{\class{purple}{\theta}} \frac{\sin(\class{purple}{\theta})}{\class{red}{r}})\qty(\cos(\theta) \class{red}{\vu{r}} - \sin(\theta) \class{purple}{\vu{\theta}}) \\
&\quad {}+ \qty(\pdv{f}{\class{red}{r}} \sin(\class{purple}{\theta}) + \pdv{f}{\class{purple}{\theta}} \frac{\cos(\class{purple}{\theta})}{\class{red}{r}})\qty(\sin(\theta) \class{red}{\vu{r}} + \cos(\theta) \class{purple}{\vu{\theta}}) \\
&\quad {}+ \pdv{f}{\posZ} \class{green}{\vu{k}} \\
&= \qty(\pdv{f}{\class{red}{r}} \cos(\class{purple}{\theta}) - \pdv{f}{\class{purple}{\theta}} \frac{\sin(\class{purple}{\theta})}{\class{red}{r}})\cos(\theta) \class{red}{\vu{r}} - \qty(\pdv{f}{\class{red}{r}} \cos(\class{purple}{\theta}) - \pdv{f}{\class{purple}{\theta}} \frac{\sin(\class{purple}{\theta})}{\class{red}{r}})\sin(\theta) \class{purple}{\vu{\theta}} \\
&\quad {}+ \qty(\pdv{f}{\class{red}{r}} \sin(\class{purple}{\theta}) + \pdv{f}{\class{purple}{\theta}} \frac{\cos(\class{purple}{\theta})}{\class{red}{r}})\sin(\theta) \class{red}{\vu{r}} + \qty(\pdv{f}{\class{red}{r}} \sin(\class{purple}{\theta}) + \pdv{f}{\class{purple}{\theta}} \frac{\cos(\class{purple}{\theta})}{\class{red}{r}})\cos(\theta) \class{purple}{\vu{\theta}} \\
&\quad {}+ \pdv{f}{\posZ} \class{green}{\vu{k}} \\
&= \qty(\pdv{f}{\class{red}{r}} \cos[2](\class{purple}{\theta}) \cancel{- \pdv{f}{\class{purple}{\theta}} \frac{\cos(\class{purple}{\theta}) \sin(\class{purple}{\theta})}{\class{red}{r}}} + \pdv{f}{\class{red}{r}} \sin[2](\class{purple}{\theta}) \cancel{ +\pdv{f}{\class{purple}{\theta}} \frac{\cos(\class{purple}{\theta}) \sin(\class{purple}{\theta})}{\class{red}{r}}})\class{red}{\vu{r}} \\
&\quad {}+ \qty(\cancel{-\pdv{f}{\class{red}{r}} \sin(\class{purple}{\theta}) \cos(\class{purple}{\theta})} + \pdv{f}{\class{purple}{\theta}} \frac{\sin[2](\class{purple}{\theta})}{\class{red}{r}} \cancel{+ \pdv{f}{\class{red}{r}} \sin(\class{purple}{\theta}) \cos(\class{purple}{\theta})} + \pdv{f}{\class{purple}{\theta}} \frac{\cos[2](\class{purple}{\theta})}{\class{red}{r}})\class{purple}{\vu{\theta}} \\
&\quad {}+ \pdv{f}{\posZ} \class{green}{\vu{k}} \\
&= \pdv{f}{\class{red}{r}} \class{red}{\vu{r}} + \frac{1}{\class{red}{r}} \pdv{f}{\class{purple}{\theta}} \class{purple}{\vu{\theta}} + \pdv{f}{\posZ} \class{green}{\vu{k}}
\end{split}
\end{equation}
$$

Thus, the gradient of a function $f$ in cylindrical coordinates is:

$$
\begin{equation}
\grad{f} = \pdv{f}{\class{red}{r}} \class{red}{\vu{r}} + \frac{1}{\class{red}{r}} \pdv{f}{\class{purple}{\theta}} \class{purple}{\vu{\theta}} + \pdv{f}{\posZ} \class{green}{\vu{k}}
\end{equation}
$$
