One-Dimensional Kinematics

One-dimensional kinematics is the study of motion in one dimension.

Kinematics vs Dynamics​

Kinematics studies how objects move, considering the various trajectories and velocities.

On the other hand, dynamics studies the causes of motion, the forces that cause objects to move.

Position​

Position is the location of an object in space. It is a vector quantity, meaning it has both magnitude and direction.

Usually, position is denoted as a function of time r(t)\vec{r}(t)r(t), where ttt is time. In one-dimensional kinematics, the position can be written as x(t)i^x(t)\hat{\vec{i}}x(t)i^. Here, i^\hat{\vec{i}}i^ is the unit vector in the direction of motion.

Displacement​

Displacement is the change in position of an object, or Δr\Delta \vec{r}Δr. It is also a vector quantity. Displacement can be expressed as follows:

Δr=r(t2)−r(t1)\Delta \vec{r} = \vec{r}(t_2) - \vec{r}(t_1)Δr=r(t2​)−r(t1​)

In one-dimensional kinematics, the displacement can be written as:

Δr=x(t2)i^−x(t1)i^≡Δxi^\Delta \vec{r} = x(t_2)\hat{\vec{i}} - x(t_1)\hat{\vec{i}} \equiv \Delta x\hat{\vec{i}}Δr=x(t2​)i^−x(t1​)i^≡Δxi^

Velocity​

Velocity is the rate of change of position with respect to time. It is a vector quantity. The SI unit of velocity is meters per second (ms−1\text{ms}^{-1}ms−1).

The average velocity, which is the average rate of change of position, is given by:

vavg=ΔrΔt\vec{v}_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta \vec{r}}{\Delta t}vavg​=ΔtΔr​

In one-dimensional kinematics, the average velocity can be written as:

vavg=ΔxΔt=x(t+Δt)−x(t)Δtv_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{x(t + \Delta t) - x(t)}{\Delta t}vavg​=ΔtΔx​=Δtx(t+Δt)−x(t)​

The instantaneous velocity is the velocity at a specific point in time. It is the limit of the average velocity as the time interval approaches zero:

v=lim⁡Δt→0x(t+Δt)−x(t)Δt=dxdtv = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{x(t + \Delta t) - x(t)}{\Delta t} = \frac{dx}{dt}v=Δt→0lim​Δtx(t+Δt)−x(t)​=dtdx​

The vector form of velocity is:

Acceleration​

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity with respect to time. It is a vector quantity. The SI unit of acceleration is meters per second squared (ms−2\text{ms}^{-2}ms−2).

The average acceleration, which is the average rate of change of velocity, is given by:

aavg=ΔvΔt\vec{a}_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t}aavg​=ΔtΔv​

In one-dimensional kinematics, the average acceleration can be written as:

aavg=ΔvΔt=v(t+Δt)−v(t)Δta_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{v(t + \Delta t) - v(t)}{\Delta t}aavg​=ΔtΔv​=Δtv(t+Δt)−v(t)​

Just like velocity, the instantaneous acceleration is the acceleration at a specific point in time. It is the limit of the average acceleration as the time interval approaches zero:

a=lim⁡Δt→0v(t+Δt)−v(t)Δt=dvdta = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{v(t + \Delta t) - v(t)}{\Delta t} = \frac{dv}{dt}a=Δt→0lim​Δtv(t+Δt)−v(t)​=dtdv​

The vector form of acceleration is:

a=dvdt\vec{a} = \frac{d\vec{v}}{dt}a=dtdv​

And in one-dimensional kinematics:

a=ddt(vi^)=dvdti^\vec{a} = \frac{d}{dt}(v\hat{\vec{i}}) = \frac{dv}{dt}\hat{\vec{i}}a=dtd​(vi^)=dtdv​i^

Example: Vehicles​

Imagine a train traveling at a constant velocity of 20ms−120 \text{ms}^{-1}20ms−1. Then, imagine a road parallel to the train tracks, with a car stopping at a traffic light.

The moment the traffic light turns green, the train passes the car, and the car starts accelerating at 2ms−22 \text{ms}^{-2}2ms−2. At what time does the car catch up to the train?

To solve this problem, first evaluate the position functions for both the train and the car.

For the train, the position function is:

When the car catches up to the train, that means the positions are equal, so the lines on the graph intersect.

xcar(t)=xtrain(t)12acart2=vtraintt=2vtrainacar=2×20ms−12ms−2=20s\begin{align*} x_{\text{car}}(t) &= x_{\text{train}}(t) \\ \frac{1}{2} a_{\text{car}} t^2 &= v_{\text{train}} t \\ t &= \frac{2 v_{\text{train}}}{a_{\text{car}}} \\ &= \frac{2 \times 20 \text{ms}^{-1}}{2 \text{ms}^{-2}} \\ &= 20 \text{s} \end{align*}xcar​(t)21​acar​t2t​=xtrain​(t)=vtrain​t=acar​2vtrain​​=2ms−22×20ms−1​=20s​

The car catches up to the train at t=20st = 20 \text{s}t=20s. (Technically the algebra neglected t=0t = 0t=0 as a solution, which is the initial position of the car.)

To find out how far the car has traveled, substitute t=20t = 20t=20 into the car's position function:

Non-Constant Acceleration​

For non-constant acceleration, the velocity-time graph is not a straight line.

To get the velocity, we need to make use of calculus.

a(t)=dvdt  ⟹  v(t)=∫a(t) dta(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} \implies v(t) = \int a(t) \, dta(t)=dtdv​⟹v(t)=∫a(t)dt

This is an antiderivative, which is the reverse of differentiation. For example, if a(t)=bt2a(t) = bt^2a(t)=bt2 where bbb is a constant, then:

v(t)=∫bt2 dt=13bt3+v0v(t) = \int bt^2 \, dt = \frac{1}{3} bt^3 + v_0v(t)=∫bt2dt=31​bt3+v0​

Consider an acceleration-time graph. The area under the graph is the velocity. The area is thought of as a sum of rectangles, each with a width of dtdtdt and a height of a(t)a(t)a(t). To find the area, notice that a small change in time dtdtdt incurs a small change in area, which is a rectangle and as such can be calculated as a(t)dta(t) dta(t)dt.

dArea=dv=a(t) dt  ⟹  dvdt=a(t)d\text{Area} = dv = a(t) \, dt \implies \frac{dv}{dt} = a(t)dArea=dv=a(t)dt⟹dtdv​=a(t)

And therefore,

∫tita(t) dt=v(t)+C\int_{t_i}^{t} a(t) \, dt = v(t) + C∫ti​t​a(t)dt=v(t)+C

When t=tit = t_it=ti​, the area from tit_iti​ to ttt is 0, so C=−v(ti)C = -v(t_i)C=−v(ti​). Hence,

∫tita(t) dt=v(t)−v(ti)\int_{t_i}^{t} a(t) \, dt = v(t) - v(t_i)∫ti​t​a(t)dt=v(t)−v(ti​)

This is the fundamental theorem of calculus.

Summary​

  • Position is the location of an object in space.
  • Displacement is the change in position of an object.
  • Velocity is the rate of change of position with respect to time.
  • Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity with respect to time.
  • For constant acceleration, the velocity-time graph is a straight line, and the position function is x(t)=x0+v0t+12at2x(t) = x_0 + v_0 t + \frac{1}{2} a t^2x(t)=x0​+v0​t+21​at2.
  • For non-constant acceleration, the velocity-time graph is not a straight line.
  • In differential order: position →\to→ velocity →\to→ acceleration; x→v→ax \to v \to ax→v→a.
  • In integral order: acceleration →\to→ velocity →\to→ position; a→v→xa \to v \to xa→v→x.
