Differentiation

Differentiation is the process of finding the derivative of a function.

Imagine a car moving along a road. The car's position at time ttt is given by a function s(t)s(t)s(t). Let's say we graph the car's position as a function of time:

To find the car's velocity, vvv, at time ttt, we can find the slope of the line tangent to the graph of s(t)s(t)s(t) at ttt. In this case, since it is a straight line, we can simply take two points and find ΔyΔx\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}ΔxΔy​:

Slope=ΔyΔx=y2−y1x2−x1=5.00−2.0010.00−4.00=3.006.00=12 \begin{align*} \text{Slope} &= \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \\ &= \frac{5.00 - 2.00}{10.00 - 4.00} \\ &= \frac{3.00}{6.00} \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \end{align*} Slope​=ΔxΔy​=x2​−x1​y2​−y1​​=10.00−4.005.00−2.00​=6.003.00​=21​​

The slope of the line is 12\frac{1}{2}21​, so the car's velocity at any given time ttt is 12\frac{1}{2}21​.

However, in most cases the function will not be a straight line, so we need a more general way to find the slope, which is where the derivative comes in.

Derivative from Limits​

Instead of thinking about the slope of a function as a whole, a derivative deals with the slope at a singular point. Let's say we have a function f(x)f(x)f(x):

Suppose we want to find the slope of the function at x=3x = 3x=3. What this means is that if we construct line that's tangent to the graph of f(x)f(x)f(x) at x=3x = 3x=3, what is the slope of that line?

To find the slope of the tangent line at x=3x = 3x=3, we can take two points on the graph of f(x)f(x)f(x) that are very close to x=3x = 3x=3. Let's say we take x=3x = 3x=3 and x=3+Δxx = 3 + \Delta xx=3+Δx:

Slope=ΔyΔx=3.20−1.804.00−3.00=1.401.00=1.40 \begin{align*} \text{Slope} &= \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} \\ &= \frac{3.20 - 1.80}{4.00 - 3.00} \\ &= \frac{1.40}{1.00} \\ &= 1.40 \end{align*} Slope​=ΔxΔy​=4.00−3.003.20−1.80​=1.001.40​=1.40​

From the above, we can see that the slope of the tangent line at x=3x = 3x=3 can be expressed as follows:

Slope=ΔyΔx=f(3+Δx)−f(3)Δx\text{Slope} = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{f(3 + \Delta x) - f(3)}{\Delta x}Slope=ΔxΔy​=Δxf(3+Δx)−f(3)​

As Δx\Delta xΔx approaches 000, the slope of the tangent line approaches a certain value. This value is the derivative of the function f(x)f(x)f(x) at x=3x = 3x=3.

As such, the derivative of a function f(x)f(x)f(x) at a point xxx is defined as:

f′(x)=lim⁡Δx→0f(x+Δx)−f(x)Δxf'(x) = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{f(x + \Delta x) - f(x)}{\Delta x}f′(x)=Δx→0lim​Δxf(x+Δx)−f(x)​
Symbols
  • Some textbooks use hhh instead of Δx\Delta xΔx for some reason.
  • The derivative of a function f(x)f(x)f(x) is often denoted as f′(x)f'(x)f′(x), pronounced "f prime of x".
  • dydx\frac{dy}{dx}dxdy​ is known as Leibniz notation, while f′(x)f'(x)f′(x) is known as Lagrange notation.

Delta and d​

In the above, we used Δx\Delta xΔx to represent a small change in xxx, and likewise for Δy\Delta yΔy. As the change approaches 000, another symbol is used to represent this infinitesimally small change: dxdxdx and dydydy.

When using ddd, the concept of it approaching 000 is "built-in" to the symbol, so we can write the derivative as:

f′(x)=dydxf'(x) = \frac{dy}{dx}f′(x)=dxdy​

If we want to differentiate, let's say, x2x^2x2, we can write it as:

ddxx2 or d(x2)dx\frac{d}{dx} x^2 \text{ or } \frac{d(x^2)}{dx}dxd​x2 or dxd(x2)​

The fraction on the left is the differential operator.

While some textbooks suggest that dydx\frac{dy}{dx}dxdy​ shouldn't be thought of as a fraction, it's often much more intuitive to think of it as one. Even though it's not a fraction in the strictest sense, it behaves like one in many ways, and it is indeed a slope after all. Furthermore, when dydydy and dxdxdx are seen by themselves, they are much easier to understand. This is important especially in implicit differentiation.

Differentiation of Sums of Functions​

The derivative of a sum of functions is the sum of the derivatives of the functions. To demonstrate this, let's find the derivative of the function f(x)=15x2+13xf(x) = \frac{1}{5} x^2 + \frac{1}{3} xf(x)=51​x2+31​x.

Once again, let's find the derivative at x=3x = 3x=3. Look at the figure below:

The straight line is the 13x\frac{1}{3} x31​x part, and the curve is the whole function. The two arrows represent that at that point, the total value of the function is the sum of the two terms, with each arrow representing one term.

Recall that to take the slope, you imagine another point some distance dxdxdx away and measure how much yyy changes:

The key idea is that this green dydydy can be "split" into two parts: one from the 15x2\frac{1}{5} x^251​x2 term and one from the 13x\frac{1}{3} x31​x term.

Therefore:

ddx(15x2+13x)=ddx(15x2)+ddx(13x)\frac{d}{dx} \left( \frac{1}{5} x^2 + \frac{1}{3} x \right) = \frac{d}{dx} \left( \frac{1}{5} x^2 \right) + \frac{d}{dx} \left( \frac{1}{3} x \right)dxd​(51​x2+31​x)=dxd​(51​x2)+dxd​(31​x)

Or more generally,

ddx(f(x)+g(x))=ddxf(x)+ddxg(x)\begin{equation} \frac{d}{dx} \left( f(x) + g(x) \right) = \frac{d}{dx} f(x) + \frac{d}{dx} g(x) \end{equation}dxd​(f(x)+g(x))=dxd​f(x)+dxd​g(x)​​

Chain Rule​

The chain rule is a way to differentiate composite functions.

Let's say we have two functions f(x)f(x)f(x) and g(x)g(x)g(x), and we want to find the derivative of f(g(x))f(g(x))f(g(x)).

To do this, we can think of f(g(x))f(g(x))f(g(x)) as a function of g(x)g(x)g(x), and then differentiate that function with respect to xxx.

Conceptually, the chain rule can be thought of as a way to "break down" the function into smaller parts.

Imagine a knob that you can turn to change xxx, and that turns another knob that changes g(x)g(x)g(x), which in turn changes f(g(x))f(g(x))f(g(x)).

To measure how much f(g(x))f(g(x))f(g(x)) changes when you turn the first knob, you can measure how much g(x)g(x)g(x) changes, and then how much f(g(x))f(g(x))f(g(x)) changes when you change g(x)g(x)g(x).

Another way to think of it is through algebraic manipulation, assuming y=f(g(x))y = f(g(x))y=f(g(x)):

dydx=dydg⋅dgdx\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{dg} \cdot \frac{dg}{dx}dxdy​=dgdy​⋅dxdg​

Powers​

The derivative of a power function is:

ddxxn=n⋅xn−1\frac{d}{dx} x^n = n \cdot x^{n - 1}dxd​xn=n⋅xn−1

To show this, let's make a general power function f(x)=xnf(x) = x^nf(x)=xn and apply the limit to it:

f′(x)=lim⁡Δx→0(x+Δx)n−xnΔxf'(x) = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{(x + \Delta x)^n - x^n}{\Delta x}f′(x)=Δx→0lim​Δx(x+Δx)n−xn​

The (x+Δx)n(x + \Delta x)^n(x+Δx)n term is a binomial. In an expanded binomial, you're essentially choosing every possible combination of terms from the two binomials. For example, (x+a)3(x + a)^3(x+a)3 expands to x3+3x2a+3xa2+a3x^3 + 3x^2a + 3xa^2 + a^3x3+3x2a+3xa2+a3 because:

  • There's one way to choose x3a0x^3a^0x3a0 from xxx and aaa: x⋅x⋅xx \cdot x \cdot xx⋅x⋅x
  • There are three ways to choose x2a1x^2a^1x2a1 from xxx and aaa: x⋅x⋅ax \cdot x \cdot ax⋅x⋅a, x⋅a⋅xx \cdot a \cdot xx⋅a⋅x, and a⋅x⋅xa \cdot x \cdot xa⋅x⋅x
  • There are three ways to choose x1a2x^1a^2x1a2 from xxx and aaa: x⋅a⋅ax \cdot a \cdot ax⋅a⋅a, a⋅x⋅aa \cdot x \cdot aa⋅x⋅a, and a⋅a⋅xa \cdot a \cdot xa⋅a⋅x
  • There's one way to choose a3x0a^3x^0a3x0 from xxx and aaa: a⋅a⋅aa \cdot a \cdot aa⋅a⋅a

The formalization of this is the binomial theorem:

(x+a)n=∑k=0n(nk)xn−kak(x + a)^n = \sum_{k = 0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} x^{n - k} a^k(x+a)n=k=0∑n​(kn​)xn−kak

Where (nk)\binom{n}{k}(kn​) is the binomial coefficient, which is what you get when you choose kkk items from nnn items.

Applying this to the limit:

f′(x)=lim⁡Δx→0(x+Δx)n−xnΔx=lim⁡Δx→0∑k=0n(nk)xn−k(Δx)k−xnΔx=lim⁡Δx→01Δx(xn+(n1)xn−1(Δx)1+…+(nn)x0(Δx)n−xn)\begin{align*} f'(x) &= \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{(x + \Delta x)^n - x^n}{\Delta x} \\ &= \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{\sum_{k = 0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} x^{n - k} (\Delta x)^k - x^n}{\Delta x} \\ &= \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{1}{\Delta x} \left( \cancel{x^n} + \binom{n}{1} x^{n - 1} (\Delta x)^1 + \ldots + \binom{n}{n} x^0 (\Delta x)^n - \cancel{x^n} \right) \\ \end{align*}f′(x)​=Δx→0lim​Δx(x+Δx)n−xn​=Δx→0lim​Δx∑k=0n​(kn​)xn−k(Δx)k−xn​=Δx→0lim​Δx1​(xn+(1n​)xn−1(Δx)1+…+(nn​)x0(Δx)n−xn)​

The key part is that after canceling out the xnx^nxn terms, the only term that remains is the one where k=1k = 1k=1. This is because the other terms have a Δx\Delta xΔx in them, which will go to 000 as Δx\Delta xΔx approaches 000.

Therefore, the derivative of xnx^nxn is:

ddxxn=n⋅xn−1\frac{d}{dx} x^n = n \cdot x^{n - 1}dxd​xn=n⋅xn−1
What about non-integers?

The above formula works for all real powers, not just integers. Here's a proof.

Let's say we have a power function:

f(x)=xr=exp⁡(rln⁡x)where r∈Rf(x) = x^r = \exp(r \ln{x}) \quad \text{where } r \in \mathbb{R}f(x)=xr=exp(rlnx)where r∈R

To find the derivative, we can use the chain rule:

f′(x)=ddxexp⁡(rln⁡x)=exp⁡(rln⁡x)⋅ddx(rln⁡x)=xr⋅ddx(rln⁡x)=xr⋅r⋅1x=r⋅xr−1\begin{align*} f'(x) &= \frac{d}{dx} \exp(r \ln{x}) \\ &= \exp(r \ln{x}) \cdot \frac{d}{dx} (r \ln{x}) \\ &= x^r \cdot \frac{d}{dx} (r \ln{x}) \\ &= x^r \cdot r \cdot \frac{1}{x} \\ &= r \cdot x^{r - 1} \end{align*}f′(x)​=dxd​exp(rlnx)=exp(rlnx)⋅dxd​(rlnx)=xr⋅dxd​(rlnx)=xr⋅r⋅x1​=r⋅xr−1​

A few things to note:

  • This relies on the fact that the derivative of ln⁡x\ln{x}lnx is 1x\frac{1}{x}x1​.
  • This also relies on the fact that the derivative of exp⁡x\exp{x}expx is itself exp⁡x\exp{x}expx.

Differentiability and Continuity​

The differentiability of a function at a point refers to whether the function has a derivative at that point.

The important thing to note is this: if a function f(x)f(x)f(x) is differentiable at x=ax = ax=a, then it is continuous at x=ax = ax=a.

However, the converse is not necessarily true: a function can be continuous at a point but not differentiable at that point.

As previously mentioned, a function is continuous at a point if:

lim⁡x→af(x)=f(a)\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)x→alim​f(x)=f(a)

A function is differentiable at a point if:

f′(a)=lim⁡Δx→0f(a+Δx)−f(a)Δxf'(a) = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{f(a + \Delta x) - f(a)}{\Delta x}f′(a)=Δx→0lim​Δxf(a+Δx)−f(a)​

To prove that differentiability implies continuity, we'll use a slightly different form of the limit definition of the derivative:

f′(a)=lim⁡x→af(x)−f(a)x−af'(a) = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a}f′(a)=x→alim​x−af(x)−f(a)​

Let's suppose we want to find this limit below, assuming f(x)f(x)f(x) is differentiable at x=ax = ax=a:

lim⁡x→af(x)−f(a)=lim⁡x→af(x)−f(a)x−a⋅(x−a)=lim⁡x→af(x)−f(a)x−a⋅lim⁡x→a(x−a)=f′(a)⋅0=0lim⁡x→af(x)−f(a)=0∴lim⁡x→af(x)=f(a)\begin{align*} \lim_{x \to a} f(x) - f(a) &= \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a} \cdot (x - a) \\ &= \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a} \cdot \lim_{x \to a} (x - a) \\ &= f'(a) \cdot 0 \\ &= 0 \\ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) - f(a) &= 0 \\ \therefore \lim_{x \to a} f(x) &= f(a) \end{align*}x→alim​f(x)−f(a)x→alim​f(x)−f(a)∴x→alim​f(x)​=x→alim​x−af(x)−f(a)​⋅(x−a)=x→alim​x−af(x)−f(a)​⋅x→alim​(x−a)=f′(a)⋅0=0=0=f(a)​

Hence, if f(x)f(x)f(x) is differentiable at x=ax = ax=a, then it is continuous at x=ax = ax=a.

Higher Order Derivatives​

The derivative of a function is itself a function, so it can be differentiated again.

The second derivative of a function f(x)f(x)f(x) is denoted as f′′(x)f''(x)f′′(x) or d2dx2f(x)\frac{d^2}{dx^2} f(x)dx2d2​f(x), the latter being sort of a notation hack.

The second derivative is the derivative of the first derivative, and the third derivative is the derivative of the second derivative, and so on.

Higher order derivatives can be denoted as f(n)(x)f^{(n)}(x)f(n)(x), where nnn is the order of the derivative.

Implicit Differentiation and the Natural Log​

Implicit differentiation is a way to differentiate functions that are not explicitly defined.

For example, consider the equation of a circle:

x2+y2=r2x^2 + y^2 = r^2x2+y2=r2

To differentiate this, we can treat rrr as some constant and x2+y2x^2 + y^2x2+y2 as some function P(x,y)P(x, y)P(x,y). Just like how we took small changes in xxx to find the derivative of x2x^2x2, we can take small changes in xxx and yyy to find the derivative of P(x,y)P(x, y)P(x,y).

dP=2x⋅dx+2y⋅dydP = 2x \cdot dx + 2y \cdot dydP=2x⋅dx+2y⋅dy

Remember that PPP should be constant, so dP=0dP = 0dP=0.

We can also use implicit differentiation to find the derivative of the natural logarithm.

Given y=ln⁡xy = \ln{x}y=lnx, we can rewrite this as ey=xe^y = xey=x. Then, we can once again think of moving along both the xxx and yyy axes and find the derivative of eye^yey with respect to xxx.
