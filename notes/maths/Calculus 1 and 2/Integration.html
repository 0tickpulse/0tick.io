Integration

Integration is the process of finding the integral of a function. It is the reverse process of differentiation, and is used to find the area under a curve, the volume of a solid, and many other quantities.

Area Under a Curve through Accumulation of Infinitesimal Areas​

Imagine a curve like this one:

This is a curve defined by the function v(x)=x(5−x)v(x) = x(5 - x)v(x)=x(5−x). Let this be the velocity of a vehicle at time ttt (imagine it as a reading on the speedometer).

From differentiation we know that the derivative of the displacement function s(t)s(t)s(t) is the velocity function v(t)v(t)v(t). In other words, dsdt(t)=v(t)\frac{ds}{dt}(t) = v(t)dtds​(t)=v(t).

If we want to find the displacement of the vehicle at time ttt, we need to find a function s(t)s(t)s(t) whose derivative is v(t)v(t)v(t). This is known as the antiderivative of v(t)v(t)v(t).

A visual representation of this is the area under the curve of v(t)v(t)v(t) from 000 to ttt. Since you can't just find the area (for now), you can approximate it by finding the area of rectangles.

A visualization is shown below:

Area Under a Curve

Number of rectangles: 10

For each rectangle at time tit_iti​, the width is Δt\Delta tΔt and the height is v(ti)v(t_i)v(ti​). Hence, the area of the rectangle is v(ti)Δtv(t_i) \Delta tv(ti​)Δt. The total area is the sum of the areas of all the rectangles:

Area=∑iv(ti)Δt\text{Area} = \sum_{i} v(t_i) \Delta tArea=i∑​v(ti​)Δt

Because Δt→0\Delta t \to 0Δt→0, the sum becomes a "continuous sum". This is what we call an integral:

Area=∫0T1v(t)dt\text{Area} = \int_{0}^{T_1} v(t) dtArea=∫0T1​​v(t)dt

Accumulation of Areas and the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus​

Now, let's consider how to actually compute the area. Recall that the area of the velocity curve is equivalent to the displacement of the vehicle.

In other words:

s(T1)=∫0T1v(t)dts(T_1) = \int_{0}^{T_1} v(t) dts(T1​)=∫0T1​​v(t)dt

Additionally, recall that to find the slope of a function, we took an infinitesimal change in xxx and found the change in yyy. We can apply the same principle here: if we increment ttt, then we can calculate the change in the area.

At time T1T_1T1​, if we increment ttt by Δt\Delta tΔt, then the area will be added by another rectangle, as shown above in green. The width of the rectangle is Δt\Delta tΔt and the height is v(T1)v(T_1)v(T1​). Therefore:

Δs=v(T1)Δt\Delta s = v(T_1) \Delta tΔs=v(T1​)Δt

And if we rearrange:

ΔsΔt=v(t)\frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t} = v(t)ΔtΔs​=v(t)

And as Δt→0\Delta t \to 0Δt→0:

dsdt=v(t)\frac{ds}{dt} = v(t)dtds​=v(t)

We have derived something known as the fundamental theorem of calculus:

dsdt=v(t)s(T1)=∫0T1v(t)dt\begin{aligned} \frac{ds}{dt} &= v(t) \\ s(T_1) &= \int_{0}^{T_1} v(t) dt \end{aligned}dtds​s(T1​)​=v(t)=∫0T1​​v(t)dt​

The Antiderivative​

As shown above, the fundamental theorem of calculus states that the antiderivative of a function is the area under the curve of the function.

To find the antiderivative of a function, we take what we know about differentiation and work backwards.

For example, consider the antiderivative of xnx^nxn. Recall that the derivative of xax^axa is axa−1ax^{a - 1}axa−1. To get rid of the coefficient, simply divide by aaa; so the derivative of xa/ax^a/axa/a is xa−1x^{a - 1}xa−1.

If we posit xn=xa−1x^n = x^{a - 1}xn=xa−1, then a−1=na - 1 = na−1=n and a=n+1a = n + 1a=n+1.

Therefore, the antiderivative of xnx^nxn is xn+1/(n+1)x^{n + 1}/(n + 1)xn+1/(n+1).

Antiderivative Notation​

The antiderivative of a function f(x)f(x)f(x) is denoted as ∫f(x)dx\int f(x) dx∫f(x)dx. When we took the antiderivative of xnx^nxn, we can write it as:

∫xndx=xn+1n+1\int x^n dx = \frac{x^{n + 1}}{n + 1}∫xndx=n+1xn+1​

HOWEVER, we are missing something. Let's say n=4n = 4n=4. Then the antiderivative of x4x^4x4 is x5/5x^5/5x5/5.

Recall that the antiderivative is the function whose derivative is the original function.

Since the derivative of x5/5x^5/5x5/5 is 5x45x^45x4, it is the antiderivative of x4x^4x4. However, the derivative of x5/5+1x^5/5 + 1x5/5+1 is 5x45x^45x4 as well, as well as the derivative of x5/5+2x^5/5 + 2x5/5+2, and so on, since the derivative of a constant is 000.

Visually, this looks like moving the antiderivative up and down the yyy-axis:

We can denote this constant as CCC. Therefore, we can revise our antiderivative as:

∫xndx=xn+1n+1+C\int x^n dx = \frac{x^{n + 1}}{n + 1} + C∫xndx=n+1xn+1​+C

We have now found the antiderivative of xnx^nxn, known as the power rule of integration.

Definite Integrals​

Now let's connect the antiderivative to the area under the curve. Using what we know so far:

∫0T1v(t)dt=s(T1)+C\int_{0}^{T_1} v(t) dt = s(T_1) + C∫0T1​​v(t)dt=s(T1​)+C

However, what do we set CCC as? It can't be any constant, since the area under the curve is a specific value. To solve this conundrum, we introduce the concept of a definite integral. Let's say we want to find the area under the curve of v(t)v(t)v(t) from 000 to 000. This is obviously 000, so:

∫00v(t)dt=0=s(0)+C\int_{0}^{0} v(t) dt = 0 = s(0) + C∫00​v(t)dt=0=s(0)+C

Therefore, C=−s(0)C = -s(0)C=−s(0). This is the basis of the definite integral:

∫0T1v(t)dt=s(T1)−s(0)\int_{0}^{T_1} v(t) dt = s(T_1) - s(0)∫0T1​​v(t)dt=s(T1​)−s(0)

We can also replace 000 with any arbitrary constant. For example, if we want to find the area under the curve of v(t)v(t)v(t) from T0T_0T0​ to T1T_1T1​:

∫T0T1v(t)dt=s(T1)−s(T0)\int_{T_0}^{T_1} v(t) dt = s(T_1) - s(T_0)∫T0​T1​​v(t)dt=s(T1​)−s(T0​)

Notice that the area under the curve is derived solely from the endpoints T0T_0T0​ and T1T_1T1​, and nothing in between.

Average Value of a Function​

Usually, when we talk about the average of a set of numbers, we think of a discrete set of values, like the average of 1,2,3,4,51, 2, 3, 4, 51,2,3,4,5. Calculus is all about extending discrete concepts to continuous ones, so we can also find the average value of a continuous function.

Let's once again consider the velocity function v(t)v(t)v(t), and split it into nnn intervals of width Δt\Delta tΔt.

The average value of a set of numbers is the sum of the numbers divided by the count:

Average=∑iv(ti)n\text{Average} = \frac{\sum_{i} v(t_i)}{n}Average=n∑i​v(ti​)​

Let's consider what nnn is in a discrete context. It is the number of intervals (Δt\Delta tΔt) we split the function into. Therefore, it can be represented as the length of the interval divided by Δt\Delta tΔt:

n=T1−T0Δt\begin{equation} n = \frac{T_1 - T_0}{\Delta t} \end{equation}n=ΔtT1​−T0​​​​

Therefore, the average value of a function is:

Average=∑iv(ti)T1−T0Δt=∑iv(ti)ΔtT1−T0\text{Average} = \frac{\sum_{i} v(t_i)}{\frac{T_1 - T_0}{\Delta t}} = \frac{\sum_{i} v(t_i) \Delta t}{T_1 - T_0}Average=ΔtT1​−T0​​∑i​v(ti​)​=T1​−T0​∑i​v(ti​)Δt​

The numerator should look familiar: it is the area under the curve of v(t)v(t)v(t) from T0T_0T0​ to T1T_1T1​.

Therefore, the average value of a function is:

Average=∫T0T1v(t)dtT1−T0=s(T1)−s(T0)T1−T0\text{Average} = \frac{\int_{T_0}^{T_1} v(t) dt}{T_1 - T_0} = \frac{s(T_1) - s(T_0)}{T_1 - T_0}Average=T1​−T0​∫T0​T1​​v(t)dt​=T1​−T0​s(T1​)−s(T0​)​

Riemann Sums​

When we approximated the area under the curve of v(t)v(t)v(t), we used rectangles. Now we will go into more detail about this process.

What we did is known as a Riemann sum. Let's once again consider the velocity function v(t)v(t)v(t):

Riemann sums can be categorized into different types:

  • The left Riemann sum uses the left endpoint of each interval as the height of the rectangle.
  • The right Riemann sum uses the right endpoint of each interval as the height of the rectangle.
  • The midpoint Riemann sum uses the midpoint of each interval as the height of the rectangle.
  • The trapezoidal Riemann sum uses a trapezoid instead of a rectangle.

The general procedure for a Riemann sum is as follows:

  1. Identify Δt\Delta tΔt. As we know, the area of each rectangle is v(ti)Δtv(t_i) \Delta tv(ti​)Δt. In the interval [T0,T1][T_0, T_1][T0​,T1​], since there are nnn rectangles, the width of each rectangle is Δt=T1−T0n\Delta t = \frac{T_1 - T_0}{n}Δt=nT1​−T0​​.

  2. Identify tit_iti​. As shown in the graph, tit_iti​ can be represented as T0T_0T0​ plus all the arrows pointing to the right. Therefore, ti=T0+iΔtt_i = T_0 + i \Delta tti​=T0​+iΔt.

  3. Using the above values, identify the area of each iiith rectangle. The area of each rectangle can be represented as v(T0+iΔt)Δtv(T_0 + i \Delta t) \Delta tv(T0​+iΔt)Δt.

  4. Identify iii and sum all the areas. The total area is the sum of all the areas of the rectangles:

    Area=∑iv(T0+iΔt)Δt=Δt∑iv(T0+iΔt)\text{Area} = \sum_{i} v(T_0 + i \Delta t) \Delta t = \Delta t \sum_{i} v(T_0 + i \Delta t)Area=i∑​v(T0​+iΔt)Δt=Δti∑​v(T0​+iΔt)

    Identifying the bounds of the sum depends on the type of Riemann sum used:

    • For a left Riemann sum, start at i=0i = 0i=0 and end at i=n−1i = n - 1i=n−1.
    • For a right Riemann sum, start at i=1i = 1i=1 and end at i=ni = ni=n.
    • For a midpoint Riemann sum, start at i=0.5i = 0.5i=0.5 and end at i=n−0.5i = n - 0.5i=n−0.5.

    For example, the left Riemann sum can be represented as:

    Area=∑i=0n−1v(T0+iΔt)Δt=Δt∑i=0n−1v(T0+iΔt)\text{Area} = \sum_{i = 0}^{n - 1} v(T_0 + i \Delta t) \Delta t = \Delta t \sum_{i = 0}^{n - 1} v(T_0 + i \Delta t)Area=i=0∑n−1​v(T0​+iΔt)Δt=Δti=0∑n−1​v(T0​+iΔt)

Trapezoidal Rule​

The trapezoidal Riemann sum is a more accurate approximation of the area under the curve than the rectangular Riemann sums.

Recall that the area of a trapezoid is 12h(b1+b2)\frac{1}{2} h (b_1 + b_2)21​h(b1​+b2​), where hhh is the height and b1b_1b1​ and b2b_2b2​ are the bases.

In this case, if we split the area into trapezoids, hhh is Δt\Delta tΔt and b1b_1b1​ and b2b_2b2​ are v(ti)v(t_i)v(ti​) and v(ti+1)v(t_{i + 1})v(ti+1​) respectively. Therefore, the area of each trapezoid is 12Δt(v(ti)+v(ti+1))\frac{1}{2} \Delta t (v(t_i) + v(t_{i + 1}))21​Δt(v(ti​)+v(ti+1​)).

The total area is the sum of all the areas of the trapezoids:

Area=∑i=0n−112Δt(v(T0+iΔt)+v(T0+(i+1)Δt))=Δt2∑i=0n−1v(T0+iΔt)+v(T0+(i+1)Δt)\text{Area} = \sum_{i = 0}^{n - 1} \frac{1}{2} \Delta t (v(T_0 + i \Delta t) + v(T_0 + (i + 1) \Delta t)) = \frac{\Delta t}{2} \sum_{i = 0}^{n - 1} v(T_0 + i \Delta t) + v(T_0 + (i + 1) \Delta t)Area=i=0∑n−1​21​Δt(v(T0​+iΔt)+v(T0​+(i+1)Δt))=2Δt​i=0∑n−1​v(T0​+iΔt)+v(T0​+(i+1)Δt)

If we let n→∞n \to \inftyn→∞, then we don't need to use the trapezoidal rule, as the Riemann sum will converge to the actual area under the curve no matter what is used.

Integral as a Limit of Riemann Sums​

If we let n→∞n \to \inftyn→∞, then the Riemann sum will converge to the actual area under the curve.

Therefore, we can generalize the area under the curve as:

∫T0T1v(t)dt=lim⁡n→∞∑i=0n−1v(T0+iΔt)Δt\int_{T_0}^{T_1} v(t) dt = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i = 0}^{n - 1} v(T_0 + i \Delta t) \Delta t∫T0​T1​​v(t)dt=n→∞lim​i=0∑n−1​v(T0​+iΔt)Δt
Proof using Epsilon-Delta

Firstly, the above can be formalized into a theorem:

The proof of this theorem is quite complex, but it can be done using the ε\varepsilonε-δ\deltaδ definition of a limit.

  1. Definitions

    • Partition: A partition PPP of [a,b][a, b][a,b] is a finite set of points x0,x1,⋯ ,xnx_0, x_1, \cdots, x_nx0​,x1​,⋯,xn​ such that a=x0<x1<⋯<xn=ba = x_0 < x_1 < \cdots < x_n = ba=x0​<x1​<⋯<xn​=b.

      R(f,P,{ti})=∑f(ti)(xi−xi−1)R(f, P, \{t_i\}) = \sum f(t_i) (x_i - x_{i - 1})R(f,P,{ti​})=∑f(ti​)(xi​−xi−1​)

    These definitions are a formalization of the Riemann sum we have been discussing. What it's saying is that you can choose any intervals inside [a,b][a, b][a,b], then choose any point as the height of the rectangle, and the sum of all the areas of the rectangles will be the Riemann sum. This definition helps because it is independent of the type of the Riemann sum.

Accumulation Functions: Functions Defined by Integrals​

A function can be defined by an integral. For example, consider the following function:

f(x)=∫−4xt2dtf(x) = \int_{-4}^{x} t^2 dtf(x)=∫−4x​t2dt

This is known as an accumulation function. The reason it's called an accumulation function is because of what we've discussed above; treating integrals as an accumulation of areas.

The derivative of an accumulation function can be derived using the fundamental theorem of calculus:

dfdx(x)=ddx∫−4xt2dt=x2\begin{align*} \frac{df}{dx}(x) &= \frac{d}{dx} \int_{-4}^{x} t^2 dt \\ &= x^2 \end{align*}dxdf​(x)​=dxd​∫−4x​t2dt=x2​

Sometimes the upper bound of the integral isn't just xxx, but a function of xxx. For example:

f(x)=∫−4sin⁡(x)t2dtf(x) = \int_{-4}^{\sin(x)} t^2 dtf(x)=∫−4sin(x)​t2dt
